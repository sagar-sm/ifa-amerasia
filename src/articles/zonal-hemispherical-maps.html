Zonal Hemispherical Maps

Here and on the other side of the Habsburg imperial coat of arms we find two maps in hemispheric projection, each representing one half of a globe, an alternative projection to the unitary cordiform projection of the large mappamundi below. Both these hemispheric maps and the main map are clearly marked by five latitude divisions (painted in red both here and on the large map below) that serve to divide the world up into five climatic zones, or climata, a system of organizing the world’s regions developed by ancient geographers going back at least to Parmenides and most influentially expounded by the fifth-century CE writer Macrobius in his Commentary on Scipio’s Dream. Above the arctic and below the antarctic circles are two frigid zones, regions of excessive cold that cannot support life. Between the tropics of Cancer and Capricorn on either side of the equator is the torrid zone, a region of raging heat that, according to Parmenides and then Macrobius’s many medieval readers, was also inhospitable to life and uninhabited. Between the torrid and frigid zones are the temperate zones, the inhabited and civilized parts of the world. The known inhabited world was in the northern temperate zone, leaving it a matter of speculation whether in the antipodes there were parallel civilizations as yet unknown. Along the left edge of the Vopel map we see each of these zones clearly labeled along the appropriate latitudes. Nicolás Wey Gómez showed that this thinking about latitude rather than longitude directly motivated Columbus’s observations. Columbus had set out to discover the equatorial torrid zone to prove, among other things, that they were temperate and habitable. The discovery that the tropics were in fact fertile and inhabited made possible descriptions of the Caribbean and its people that corresponded in many ways to descriptions of India, thought to occupy the same latitude.[1]

The two hemispheric maps are flanked by portraits of significant cosmographers of Antiquity. On either side of the Western hemisphere with the New World are Solinus and Strabo; on either side of the Eastern hemisphere with Europe, Africa, and most of Asia are Pomponius Mela and Ptolemy. In 1520 Peter Apianus published a world map that closely followed Waldseemüller’s 1507 map, with its representation of the New World and a landmass labelled America. A cosmographer celebrated by the Emperor Charles V, Apianus was a significant precursor for Vopel. As the influential Apianus 1520 map was published together with editions of Solinus’ Polyhistor (1520) and of Pomponius Mela’s De Situ Orbis (1522),[2] it is possible that these associations prompted the inclusion of these two geographers on Vopel’s map, which was intended to be a much updated and more elaborate world map to replace that of Apianus.

The inclusion of the more famous Strabo and Ptolemy hardly requires explanation, though their placement in relation to the hemispheres is telling. Strabo is appropriately placed closest to the latest New World lands, a position appropriate for one who had argued that the geographer need only represent those places known to him, without needing to connect the dots into closed entities such as a singular world island. An open geographical system, he proposed, would make clearer the limits of knowledge of the world and the fact that knowledge of the farthest lands would develop. Strabo also rejected the Parmenidean idea that the torrid zone was uninhabitable, proposing that it was temperate, fertile, and inhabited, an alternative tradition that gained increasing acceptance in the later Middle Ages in the West and ultimately informed Columbus’ effort to test the theory.[3] It is therefore appropriate that Strabo holds the globe at this edge, turning to look at the latest discoveries, while Ptolemy is placed at the other side, near the part of Asia that he had considered to be the limit of the inhabited world.
