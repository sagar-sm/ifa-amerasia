Peru Provincia

To the east of his label for Peru Provincia, under the toponym Arvaceas, Vopel tells us that “the province Peru, which is also New Spain, discovered in the year 1530, has of silver and gold what the world has of everything, and they produce a very profitable supply of grain twice in a year, and the inhabitants adequately develop trade and all civilization,” (Peru prouintia, quae et Nova Castilia/ inventa Anno M.D.iii omnium quos/ orbis habet auri atque argenti ditissima/ frumenta bis in anno producit Incolae commertia [sic] atque omnem humanita/tem satis excolunt.)

The biannual harvest was considered by some to be a feature of the tropical zone (link to entry on Zonal hemispherical maps). The fact that Europeans found Peru to be as rich in silver and gold led some Europeans to hypothesize that Ophir, the site of King Solomon’s mines otherwise located in India, was located in Peru (link to Riccio essay on Ophir). The reference to two harvests per year is an ancient idea often associated with the fertility of India. The ancient historians and indographers Megasthenes and Diodorus Siculus presented India as a land rich with veins of gold, silver, copper, iron, and tin, as well as exceedingly fertile in fruits and grains of all kinds, which grew so abundantly that they could be harvested twice a year.[1] The excessive fertility and richness of India was understood to be a direct result of its occupying the central climatic zone, or zona torrida, near the equator. Peru is shown on the map just below the equator, and accordingly participates in a climatic or zonal similarity with India (see link to zonal map of the world). Although it is presented as part of America in the new world, Novus Orbis, Peru is described here in distinctly Indian terms.

[1] Cite Megasthenes and Diodorus Siculus, and Nicolás Wey Gómez, The Tropics of Empire: Why Columbus Sailed South to the Indies (Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 2008), 214-15.
