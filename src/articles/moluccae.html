Moluccae

The Moluccas, an archipelago in what is today eastern Indonesia, were also known as the Spice Islands because of the nutmeg, mace, and cloves that were originally exclusively cultivated only on these islands. Their presence ignited the interests of a great variety of European colonial powers in the sixteenth century, who gain direct access to their riches, rather than through trading intermediaries. Vopel’s cartouche just to the south of the islands themselves discusses the Spanish arrival in the Moluccas.

The Moluccas were at the heart of the insular mentality that informed Amerasian conceptions of the world (link to Archipelagus entry). A variety of authors and sources strove to show that the islands of the “Indies”, East and West, were part of the same archipelago. Various editions of Johann Boemus’s Omnium Gentium Mores, Leges et Ritus (Augsburg, 1520), for instance—one of the most popular sixteenth-century texts on geography and ethnography--placed the islands of Borneo, the Philippines, and the Moluccas in its fourth book on the West Indies or New World.[1] As Ricardo Padrón among others has shown, a great variety of Spanish historians and cosmographers were eager to conflate the islands of the Caribbean and those of the Moluccas, or to assert that the distance between them was short, in order to support Spanish claims to the Spice Islands.[2]

[1] As one example, see Gli costumi, le leggi, et l’usanze di tutte le genti (Venice: Gironimo Giglio, 1558).

[2] See Ricardo Padrón, “(Un)Inventing America: The Transpacific Indies in Oviedo and Gómara, Colonial Latin American Review 25 (2016): 16-34.
