Amazones

In Greek mythology, the Amazons were a tribe of women warriors who were located in the East, traditionally near the Scythians. Herodotus placed them on the banks of the Thermodon (Terme) River in southern Cappadocia, in what is today Turkey.[1] John Mandeville placed “the land of Amazonia, in which there are no men, only women,” somewhere between Persia and India.[2] In line with these ideas, Vopel places them just west of the Caspian Sea, also labeled by him the Scythian Gulf, Sinus Scythiacus, and somewhat to the north and east he places the Scythians.

In his Travels, Marco Polo transferred the location of the Amazons to the Arabian Sea. As he described it, on two nearby islands, “the men do not live with their wives or with any other women; but all the women live on the other island, which is called Female Island. You must know that the men of Male Island go over to Female Island and stay there for three months…After this, they return to their own island…The sons who are born and nursed by their mothers in Female Island till they are fourteen years old, when they are sent to join their fathers in Male Island.”[3] According to Polo’s revamping of the myth, the Amazons lived on an island in Asia, and as a result, became associated with Polo’s description of the fabulous wealth of the Orient.

In the wake of the Columbian voyages, these eastern places and peoples were searched for and found in the Americas. Columbus was the first to suggest that Amazons inhabited the Americas. Recounting his first voyage in his 1493 Letter to Santángel, Columbus built on Polo’s account and reported on the absence of men on the island of Matinino (Martinique). Here, “women do not follow feminine occupations but use cane bows and arrows like those of men and arm and protect themselves with plates of copper, of which they have much.”[4] Columbus’ Amazonian resonance determined the Amerasian placement of these women for many years after his voyages; his description was enough to launch a decades-long search for Amazons in the Americas. To be clear, the Amazons were not an objective for Columbus per se; rather, their presence confirmed his expectations. They were clear proof that he was close to the Orient.

The Castilian writer Garci Rodríguez de Montalvo’s chivalric romance The Adventures of Esplandián (Las Sergas de Esplandián) (1510) described Amazons on the “island” of California: “Know, that on the right hand of the Indies there is an island called California very close to the side of the Terrestrial Paradise; and it is peopled by black women, without any man among them, for they live in the manner of Amazons.[5] (link to Archipelagus entry). In 1517, the conquistador Juan Díaz reported having heard of Amazons inhabiting the northern shore of the Yucatan.[6] The governor of Cuba, Diego Velázquez, referred to the Amazons in his instructions to Hernán Cortés on the eve of his expedition to Mexico in 1519, and Cortés later noted hearing of their presence in his fourth letter of October 15, 1524 when he recounted the rumor of the explorer Cristobal de Olid, who had heard from the chiefs of Ciguatán that a short distance away, “there is an island inhabited only by women, without a single man, and that at certain times men go over from the mainland and have intercourse with them; the females born to those who conceive are kept, but the males are sent away.”[7] Indigenous descriptions of the matriarchal organization and culture of certain Peruvian tribes, in particular the customs of the “Inca Virgins”, became identified with the Amazons.[8] The Amazon river and the surrounding jungle acquired that name during Francisco de Orellana’s expedition into the area, described in the Dominican friar Gaspar de Carvajal’s 1542 account, where he recounts that at Aparia village, they began to hear of a community called by the locals coniu puyara (literally, large women), who lived further down the valley, and to whom surrounding communities offered tribute. Throughout his account, Carvajal calls them “Amazonas,” firmly and unambiguously identifying them with the legendary Asiatic warrior women.[9]

The loquacious French traveler and cosmographer André Thevet felt compelled to address the issue of Amazons in South America at length in his Singularitez de la France Antartique, autrement nommée Amérique of 1557. “Some might say that these are not real Amazons, but for myself I esteem them such, seeing that they live just as the ones in Asia did.” After offering a long account of Scythian Amazons based on ancient sources, he asks how these American Amazons got here, recounting that some say they came after the War of Troy, others say that they left Greece or Africa, chased by some cruel king. There are lots of stories about them, he admits, in the end leaving unresolved the question of the relation between the American and Asian Amazons.[10] At the end of the sixteenth century, the English explorer Sir Walter Raleigh reported hearing of Amazons in his travel narrative The Discovery of the Large, Rich, and Beautiful Empire of Guiana (1596), when he had gone in search of Guiana, an empire rich in gold rumored to exist east of Peru.[11] As with cannibals (link to Canibales entry), early modern Europeans transferred Asian visions of marvelous peoples to the Americas.

Though it remained a disputed question, the association of Asian and American Amazons became so entrenched as to enter common usage: the identifier “Amazon” for the river and surrounding region is an Amerasian trace in modern geographical language. Even after America was recognized as a separate continent, the Amazonian associations continued to imbue American identity generally. The Amazon figure became virtually emblematic of America in many allegorical images of the continents, including Adriaen Collaert’s engraving of 1588-89 based on a design by Maerten de Vos, where the continent is represented by an Amazon-like woman wearing an elaborate feathered military helmet and wielding a battle axe and a bow and arrow. Thus, even as America was being imagined as a separate continent, she was formed out of Asian-derived Amazonian imagery.
