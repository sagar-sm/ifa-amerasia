Saint Thomas, Evangelist of the Indies

Grace Walsh

In the lands of western India, Caspar Vopel tells us that "Sophronius wrote that Christ has been made known to the neighboring [and the sacis] by the work of Thomas," (Sacis et vicinis Christum/ Thomae ministerio/ praedicatum esse Sophro/nius prodidit). The story of Saint Thomas found diverse cartographic expression in the sixteenth century, but perhaps nowhere more comprehensively than Giovanni Andrea Vavassore’s 1558 edition of Vopel's world map, where the apostle can be found in three locations: in India, explicitly in his capacity as a preacher; on the southwest coast of Patagonia where a Thomasine cartographic tradition arose in the first half of the century; and most innovatively off the western coast of "Asia Magna"– a landmass that a contemporary viewer might identify as North America– where the illustration of a ship in full sail, the "Velas de S. Thomae" can be seen either debarking or embarking just above the figure of Ferdinand Magellan. As much as Thomas' triple presence in this map complicates the geographic specificity suggested by legend, in many ways it is interwoven with a medieval European imaginary of India, the plasticity of which easily encompassed an expanding world geography. The rich interpretive potential of new discoveries in both the Americas and Asia would reveal the ancient footsteps of the saint, and in turn shape the geographic definition of the places where his legacy was perceived.

In his account of the lives of the saints and apostles, the Legenda Aurea or "Golden Legend," thirteenth century writer Jacobus de Voragine opines on the meaning of Saint Thomas' name, stating that it may come from totus means, meaning "total wanderer, one who is wholly outside himself in the love and contemplation of God."[1] Though canonical scripture has little to say about Thomas' occupation after the ascension of Christ into heaven, de Voragine"s Legenda Aurea attested to fantastic stories of Thomas' travels in India, including widespread evangelization of the Indian people and surrounding miraculous events. These stories were taken up and adapted by early modern travel writers, ultimately informing explorers and missionaries who found themselves in new lands seeking validation for their discoveries, and justification for their imperial exploits. The Apostle Thomas became a mile marker, acting as a reference point by which explorers could orient themselves in unfamiliar territory, and he would eventually become a yardstick against which colonizing forces could measure the evangelical vocation of empire.

The Legenda Aurea is not the earliest text to discuss Thomas' travels in India. The account was compiled from numerous antecedents in both the antique and medieval tradition, namely the Acts of Saint Thomas and the apocryphal Historia Certaminis Apostolici (Apostolic History) attributed to a follower of Abdias of Babylon.[2] Pseudo-Abdias's Acts became the basis for both a historical and geographical tradition of Apostolic evangelization. Along with the contemporaneous Passio Santi Bartholomaei, it is thought to be an origin point for the Christianization of Ptolemy's division of India, wherein the region is divided into a tripartite model by the respective evangelization routes of the apostles Matthew, Thomas, and Bartholomew.[3] This tradition is visible in mappaemundi that predate the Legenda Aurea, such as in the Liber Floridus, a late-ninth early-tenth-century encyclopedia with a sophisticated set of geographical, cosmological, and mathematical diagrams. Its author, canon Lambert of Saint-Omer, frames his diagrammatic approach to natural history Christologically, orienting his stunning mappamundi (Fig. 1) with the furthest reaches of the east at the top, symbolizing the origin point of both the beginning and end of the world: an extant paradise that is the site of both the creation of man and the second coming of Christ at the apocalypse.

Fig. 1: Mappamundi, Lambert of Saint-Omer, Liber Floridus, 69v-70r. Herzog August Library Wolfenbüttle no. 4305.

Just below this heavenly apogee and closer than any other terrestrial domain, the three Indias, "prima", "media", and "ultima", can be found.[4]

The three Indias' proximity to paradise is perhaps predicated on this apostolic tradition, which was further embellished in the twelfth century by the mysterious Letter of Prester John, delivered, in most accounts, to the Byzantine emperor (link to Pivar essay on Prester John).[5] While the letter explicitly reiterates the three-India model, it only distinguishes one of them– "ulteriore India"– by identifying it as the resting place of the body of the Apostle Thomas.[6] The presence of the Christian priest-king Prester John in the distant lands of the east was closely aligned with the apostolic tradition, and the construction of a medieval concept of India frequently paired the two figures as saintly patriarchs and spiritual vanguards in a land otherwise populated with idolatry and monstrosity. For despite its proximity to the garden of Eden, India was also a land of monstrous marvels: Lambert of Saint-Omer illustrates this tension in his map, where we can see in the map"s labeling that India is the land of "pigmei" and "fauni" in addition to "reges gentium" (kings of nations).

This dual Indian imaginary is carried forward in both the textual and the cartographical traditions of the later Middle Ages, forming the body of evidence upon which many of the first explorers of the New World would later base their observations. Furthermore, the particular story of Saint Thomas was well known, popularized as it was by de Voragine's Legenda Aurea. This account tells of Thomas' conscription as an architect by King Gundophorus of India. While the king is away on stately business, Thomas uses the funds allocated for construction of the palace to feed the poor, and successfully evangelizes much of the royal court which enrages the king upon his return. In retribution, he subjects Thomas to numerous tortures that are prevented by miraculous intervention. Finally, the king commands Thomas to sacrifice to a pagan idol, but the idol is destroyed at Thomas' word in a demonstration of the superior power of the Christian God. He is then killed by the high priest out of vengeance.[7] This account was important and established enough to be included in the windows of Chartres Cathedral, where a full cycle of the apostle's exploits in India leading up to his eventual martyrdom can be found in a twenty-eight vitrine cycle. Based on this foundational story, popular travel accounts such as Marco Polo's Travels of the early fourteenth century and John Mandeville's Livre de Merveilles some sixty years later both refer to the site and milieu of the saint"s resting place in India. Polo states:

The body of Messer Saint Thomas the Apostle is in the province of Malabar, in a little city; for there are few men there; nor do merchants come there, for there is no merchandise that can be exported and, what's more, the place is very out of the way. It is quite true that many Christians and many Saracens come here for pilgrimage: for I tell you that the Saracens of this country have great faith in him; they say that he was Saracen and that he is a great prophet, and they call him avarian, which means "holy man"[8]

Mandeville concurs:

Fro this londe men goth many contreis and journees to a contr' that is called Mabaron. And that is a gret contry and a gret kyngdome, and ther beth many fair citees and tounes. In this londe lyth Seynt Thomas in flessh in a fair tumbe in the cit' of Calamye' In the cherche of Seynt Thomas is a greet ymage and that is a simulacre, and hit is wel y-dyght with precious stones and perles. And to that ymage men cometh fro ferre contrees with gret devocioun in pylgrimage.[9]

These accounts built on the existing legend of Saint Thomas, providing additional information about the Indian context of his martyrdom and shrine, including miracles performed by the martyr's relics, the idols of Indian people (Mandeville in particular describes an ox-headed idol[10], Polo describes idols painted black[11]), and other cultural practices such as reverence toward cows. In total, they imagine a contemporary India that is a crossroads of pagan faiths where the legacy of the saint endures as a testament to the presence and preeminence of Christian virtue amidst spiritual decline.

It is on this basis that the navigational initiatives of explorers such as Christopher Columbus, Amerigo Vespucci, and Vasco de Gama, would have anticipated signs of Christianity in newly encountered lands. Though the medieval fervor of finding Prester John tapered off in the preceding century,[12] the ongoing popularity of texts like Polo's Travels–newly published in Portugal in 1502 with a dedication to "those who now go to the indies"[13]–as well as the Legenda Aurea, assured that the legend of Thomas remained alive and well on the eve of the Age of Encounters. As such, a new round of accounts began to report the presence of Thomas in the New World. This seeming geographic confusion is elucidated by the early letters of Christopher Columbus, which frequently refer to the New World as the "Indies" and people within them as "Indians." Though the exact geography of the islands he landed on did not match the trusted descriptions in Marco Polo's text, other elements of his encounters clearly corresponded with his expectations of an immense material abundance in Asia, including the presence of highly desirable spices and cotton.[14] In addition to these worldly concerns, his copious observations about the spiritual disposition of the natives he encountered[15] reflected the evangelical ambitions that would galvanize the Spanish imperial enterprise in the years to come. For many of the missionaries who would passionately undertake the immense task of native conversion, the legend of Saint Thomas would become an important underlying mythology and a lightning rod for the moral quandary of the condition of native humanity.

Despite Saint Thomas appearing on maps as early as Martin Waldseemüller's Universalis Cosmographia of 1507, scholar Louis-André Vigneras has pinpointed a 1514 travel account printed in Augsburg as the first eyewitness report of the apostle in the landmass recently named "America".

Fig. 2: Martin Waldseemüller, Universalis cosmographia secundum Ptholomaei traditionem et Americi Vespucii alioru[m]que lustrationes, ca. 1507, Strasbourg, France.

In the Copia der Newen Zeytung ausz Prasillg Landt (Tidings out of Brazil), the author– a Portuguese or Italian trader– states:

"on this same coast they [the natives of Brazil] cherish the memory of Saint Thomas, they refer to him as the lesser god. There is, however, another god which is greater. It may be well believed that they do have memory of Saint Thomas, for it is known that the body of this saint is actually buried beyond Malacca on the Coast of Siramitl (Coromandel) in the Gulf of Ceylon. They frequently name their children Thomas in that land."[16]

This same account inspired a pamphlet by the cartographer Johann Schöner, who expanded the geographical understanding of the New World's location by stating "moreover there is only a short distance between Brazil and the Malacca region, where Saint Thomas received a martyr's crown"[17] and depicting the embarkation of Saint Thomas' ship on the southwest coast of America in his design for a globe (Fig. 3) the following year (1515), the same location as Waldseemüller eight years prior.

Fig. 3: Johann Schöner, Fragmentary Terristrial Globe Gores, 1517. Schöner Sammelband, G1015 .S43 1517.

As previously observed, this placement would live on the Vopel map of 1558.

Strangely, apart from Vopel, the apostle's name is largely absent from maps rendered of the Indian subcontinent at the time, despite the Portuguese having reached India by 1498 and their frequent interaction with Indian Christians throughout the first quarter of the sixteenth century. Notwithstanding their experience navigating in the region, and the circulation of maps like WWaldseemüller’s and Schöner's theorizing the New World as a separate continent, there was no reason to categorically dissociate Indian Christians with the accounts of Thomas and the spiritual potential in the New World. Two years before both the completion of the Magellan mission across the Pacific and the Portuguese discovery of an overgrown Christian shrine and tomb at Mylapore on the Malabar coast of India, the manufacture of a richly illuminated atlas indicated that the Portuguese worldview still held the newly discovered lands in the Atlantic as part and parcel to a greater Asian landmass. The atlas, created for King Manuel I in 1519 and now referred to as the Miller Atlas, depicts many vibrant illustrations of the inhabitants of the diverse lands of the world and shows a remarkable geographic accuracy with regards to the continental contours of the southern and southeast Asian regions. However, the stunning frontispiece (Fig. 4) depicting a full, global map shows the Asian continent continuing eastward along the edge of the world and circumscribing the Indian Ocean, connecting to a great southern "Terra Incognita" before curving upward on the opposite side to reunite with a landmass clearly labeled "Mundus Novus, Brasil."

Fig. 4: Nautical Atlas of the World, Circular World Map of the Portuguese Hemisphere and Title Page, Pedro Reinel, Jorge Reinel, Lopo Homem, and António de Holanda, The Miller Atlas,ca. 1519. 59x 41.5 cm.

Nevertheless, in 1521 excavations at Mylapore overseen by Captain Pero Lopes de Sampayo and documented firsthand by Gaspar Correia in his Lendas da Índia, uncovered a domed chapel and tomb replete with cross motifs and preserved relics, compelling evidence that the search for the Ptolemaic India that lived on in the work of Polo and Mandeville had come to a victorious end.[18]

As the image of these new worlds continued to take shape, the discoveries at Mylapore did not have conclusive impact. They were challenged by missionaries in both Spanish and Portuguese camps on the basis of other premodern accounts stating that though Thomas preached and was martyred in India, his relics were transported to the city of Edessa in Asia Minor after his death. This was an especially popular argument of Spanish skeptics like Antonio de Torquemada who took the account of Isidore of Seville as the most authoritative.[19] The existence of a venerable and privileged community of native Christians in the Indian subcontinent was enough to verify an apostolic presence but the questionable validity of the relics proved problematic to the Portuguese as well, particularly the Jesuits. Additionally, missionaries faced the question of religious lapse: when interpreting the customs of the self-identified Church of Saint Thomas, the influence of Syriac and Persian Christian traditions and the diverse interfaith milieu of southern India failed to conform to the vision of a Christian Eden prescribed by the legend. Despite the professed devotion of the Saint Thomas Christians, their practices were nonetheless seen as heretical with regards to Roman rite, perhaps reinforced by a unique regional iconography that bore similarity to Buddhist traditions in its depictions of angels and its use of animal motifs.[20] This tension may be best encapsulated by the lengthy title of one of the first significant European histories describing the Saint Thomas Christian community, António de Gouvea's Jornada do arcebispo de Goa Dom Frey Aleixo de Menezes primaz da India oriental of 1606, the full title of which can be translated as "Journey of the Archbishop of Goa, Dom Frey Aleixo de Menezes, Primate of East India, Religious of the Order of St. Augustine, when he went to the Mountains of Malabar and the places where the Ancient Christians of Saint Thomas reside and brought them away from many errors and heresies in which they were mired, and reduced them to our Holy Catholic Faith, and obedience to the Holy Roman Church, from which they had been separated for more than a thousand years."[21]

In the New World, Spanish missionaries either seeking genuine evidence of the apostle or merely seeking inroads to capturing the native imagination in order to clear a path to conversion, recognized parallels between Thomas, Moses, and Christ and native Gods, particularly the figure of Topiltzin-Quetzalcoatl in Mexico. The Dominican friar Diego Durán, who was born in New Spain in the late 1530s and worked as a missionary in both Mexico City and Oaxaca, learned Nahuatl and dedicated significant study to the Aztec religion as a means to better penetrate and overtake it. In doing so, he identified the remarkable similarities between Aztec and Christian rites that suggested biblical origins for the people of Mexico and a specific heritage of the apostolic tradition. Though he is wary of directly conflating Saint Thomas with Topiltzin-Quetzalcoatl, the mythological ruler of the Toltecs, he nonetheless catalogs their compelling similarities as an unmistakable continuum of Thomas' apostolic mission, stating:

"I would not dare affirm that Topiltzin was one of the blessed Apostles. Nevertheless, the story of his life has impressed me greatly and has led me and others to believe that, since the natives were also God’s creatures, rational and capable of salvation, He cannot have left them without a preacher of the Gospel. And if this is true, that preacher was Topiltzin, who came to this land. According to the story, he was a sculptor who carved admirable images in stone. We read that the glorious apostle Saint Thomas was a master craftsman in the same art." [22]

Topiltzin was a man who had come from a foreign country. This indicates that he appeared in this land [as a newcomer], since I can find no account of his homeland. However, it is well known, that after he arrived in this country he began to gather disciples and to build churches and altars Thus we can probably deduce that this man was an apostle whom God sent to this land and that the rest of them, the master craftsmen or wise men, were his disciples.[23]

Durán was not the first. Letters from missionaries in Brazil attest to the apostle's presence there: in 1538, Fray Bernardo de Armencia identified Thomas in the Guaraní wise man figure of Payóume, also known as Pay Thome, and in 1549 Jesuit Manuel de Nóbrega stated in his letters home "that without doubt, the apostle Thomas had preached in Brazil."[24] Furthermore, the presence of cruciform imagery in the native iconographical traditions of the Maya and the Inca confirmed for missionaries the presence of an prior Christian presence in the Americas, and was reiterated in the influential writings of chroniclers Fernández de Oviedo, Francisco López de Gómara, and Peter Martyr.[25] These monumental similarities with the findings at Mylapore contributed to an ongoing flexibility in the role India played in these missionaries' Amerasian understanding of global geography.

Despite the vastly disparate cultures encountered in new territories by the Spanish and Portuguese respectively, they found a common problem related to their anticipation of a Christian presence: the religious realities they found, whether genuinely Christian or pagan in tradition, differed vastly from the precedent set up by the legend of Saint Thomas in antique and medieval literature. This evangelical dilemma correlated with and contributed to a wider European practice of conflating new geographic discoveries. The interchangeable imaginary of South America and the Indian subcontinent long after the 1521 excavations at Mylapore was as much due to the kind of geographic continuity demonstrated by the frontispiece of Miller Atlas and the Vopel Map as it was a compression of diverse characteristics into common cultural "other" defined by its polytheism, its exoticism, its complexion and nakedness, and its proximity to the tropical abundance that the nascent nations of Europe were seeking to access. This conflation expressed itself in an artistic culture that portrayed the "Indians" of the New World and the Indians of Asia with indistinguishable physical attributes, perhaps best illustrated in the widely circulated 1508 Hans Burgkmair print series The Triumph of Maximilian I. In a print titled "The People of Calicut", depicting a procession of Indian people clothed in a manner that intertwines aspects of indigenous New World dress and attributes– feathered clothing, maize, jaguar skins– with creatures of pan-tropical origin— parrots and monkeys.

Fig. 5: Hans Burgkmair the Elder, The Triumph of Maximilian I: The People of Calicut, ca. 1516-1518. Woodblock Print, 27.3 x 37.5 cm. The British Museum, 1895,0122.388.

This iconographical model can be found in the Saint Thomas tradition; Dutch printmaker Hendrik Goltzius's late sixteenth-century cycle The Martyrdom of the Apostles after designs by painter Maarten de Vos depicted "The Martyrdom of Saint Thomas" using similar tropes.

Fig. 6: Hendrik Goltzius, The Martyrdom of the Apostles after Maarten de Vos" The Martyrdom of Saint Thomas, ca. 1590. 21.0 x 28.2 cm.

The image curiously pictures Saint Thomas twice, standing upright with his iconographical attributes of a book and a spear adjacent to a scene of his martyrdom, where Indians outfitted in draped clothes with feathered epaulettes and feathered headdresses advance menacingly to inflict the final blow. In the background a similarly dressed figure who is seated astride a camel and sheltered by what appears to be a feathered parasol gazes imperiously over the scene. The landscape is further populated by palm trees to the right and treehouses occupying a distant canopy to the left. This scene appears to represent an amalgam of cultural signifiers, each with their own encoded geographical identity that today, in the twenty-first century, we view as distinct: the feathered headdresses of indigenous American peoples, the camel of North African and Middle Eastern nomads, the parasols of the far east, and the palm trees of a pan-tropical ecology.

As the seventeenth century progressed, Thomas' role as an explicit geographic reference point waned, though his symbolic importance as exemplar for missionaries in the Reformation remained. In a monumental altarpiece conceived for the High Altar of the Barefoot Augustinians in Prague in the late 1630s, Peter Paul Rubens depicted Thomas' martyrdom at the ferocious hands of men clothed not in feather headdresses, as Goltzius would have, but in turbans and draping cloths, reflecting a more generally accurate Indian attire.

Fig. 7: Peter Paul Rubens, The Martyrdom of St. Thomas, ca. 1635-1638. Oil on canvas, 380 x 253cm. National Gallery in Prague, Šternbersky Palace, O7597.

In the shadow of a golden temple with elephant heads for column capitals and a pillar topped with a golden, horned idol, Thomas falls climactically at the feet of a stone cross like those discovered at Mylapore. The idol is reminiscent of an ivory figure of an Eastern god in the collections of Rubens' friend, French antiquarian Nicolas-Claude Fabri de Peiresc,[26] and similar to the sacred idols adorning Javanese kris daggers that began to circulate widely in central western Europe at the turn of the seventeenth century.[27] Mayu Fujikawa has suggested the influence of Johann Theodor de Bry's popular 1599 travel book India Orientalis– owned by Rubens– on his depiction of pagan idols, particularly the book's illustration of an idol in an Indian temple.[28] In the work of Rubens, Thomas' former geographic uncertainty appears to be resolved.

By Rubens' time, the apostle may have been evolving into a more general emblem of evangelical and imperial expansion. The Martyrdom of Saint Thomas was not a popular subject for European painting in the Renaissance, but Rubens had mingled the iconography of another altarpiece that perhaps indicates the changing significance of Saint Thomas as a spiritual and colonial model. Some twenty years earlier, his altarpiece depicting The Miracles of Saint Francis Xavier for the college adopted imagery reminiscent of Saint Thomas' legend to underscore the holy mission of the Jesuits.[29]

Fig. 8: Peter Paul Rubens, The Miracle of St. Francis Xavier, ca. 1617-1618. Oil on canvas, 535 x 395 cm. Kunsthistorisches Museum Vienna, Gemäldegalerie, 519.

As Xavier addresses a crowd of ill and dying from the steps of a pagan temple, a shaft of light emanates downward from the heavens, destroying a horned idol similar to the one depicted in the Saint Thomas altarpiece. The crowd itself bears a range of non-European cultural signifiers: the dark complexions of sub-Saharan Africa, Turkish turbans, a queue hairstyle that could correspond either to Chinese or Indian sources, and even a man fully dressed in a Joseon dynasty Korean banggeon and dapho, although Xavier was never known to have gone to Korea or mainland China.[30] Though there may have been genuine confusion between the costumes of different east Asian cultures of the time, the effect of the painting is not one of confusion, or conflation, but rather of symbolic convergence: the many and disparate idolators of the world gathered together to receive the word of Christ. The dialogue between the paintings points to a shift in the meaning of the Thomasine legacy. Where the apostle established the mission of the ancient church, Xavier and the Jesuits follow to manifest it in the modern age. Rather than representing a medieval vision of the east, of Eden and Prester John, an extant Christian land to be rediscovered and reclaimed, Thomas now projects into the future toward a global Christian empire precipitated by the holy office of the missionary.

Though the presence of the Saint Thomas Christians at Mylapore provided overwhelming evidence of his missionary trajectory that is still held valid by biblical historians today, the inexact definition of India both as a cultural and territorial entity in the 16th century made these findings far from definitive. The same simultaneity with which Caspar Vopel depicted Thomas, he also applied to India itself, the Indias prima, media, and ulteriore of medieval mappaemundi having evolved and survived as Vopel's Indias "maior Aethiopiae", "intra Gangem", and "extra Gangem", with the new appendage of "India orientalis" extending just north from the sails of Saint Thomas to the western edge of the map, apparently continuing onward to merge with the other side. (link to Riccio India entry) Finally, let us return to de Voragine's etymology of Thomas: totus means, total wanderer. The Legenda Aurea and its ensuing tradition localize the saint's ultimate destination in India, but not only did India's perceived parameters merely grow to assimilate new lands into an existing global model, the missionary vocation by its very nature is one of continual displacement, its goal expansive, not restrictive. The legend provided an incredibly malleable framework through which to interpret the new into Eurocentric visions of a Christian world empire. As the unimaginably vast scope of global geography came slowly into view throughout the age of exploration, the potential for apostolic discovery only multiplied, and with it the geographic understanding of the Indies.
